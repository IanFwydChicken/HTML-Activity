Cainta Relocates Creekside Families to Safer Homes

The local government of Cainta, Rizal recently relocated informal settler families living along the creek to a safer area within the municipality. This initiative aims to protect residents from the dangers of flooding during typhoons by moving them away from high‑risk creekside locations; the town government also plans to eventually restore waterways previously encroached by informal settlements.

Mayor Keith Nieto has emphasized that this relocation is part of a broader strategy to reduce disaster risk and provide long‑term housing security for affected families. Some residents opted for self‑demolition while others were assisted by the local government throughout the transfer process, and newly acquired lots will be paid off with low‑interest terms over many years.

Cainta Declares State of Calamity After Heavy Monsoon Rains

Due to severe rains and the enhancement of the Southwest Monsoon (Habagat) in 2025, Cainta, Rizal was placed under a state of calamity to hasten aid delivery and recovery work for affected residents. The disaster declaration helps with price freezes on basic goods and enables quicker government response in distributing relief and restoring essential services to flood‑hit villages.

The flooding affected multiple barangays and caused significant disruption to daily life, including displacement and loss of income for many families. By declaring a state of calamity, local authorities aimed to mobilize resources more effectively and implement both immediate and long‑term measures to mitigate flooding impacts in future rainy seasons.

Cainta Starts Esplanade Project Along Manggahan River

The Cainta local government has begun constructing a new esplanade project along the Manggahan River designed to both beautify the riverside and deter informal settlers from installing structures too close to waterways. The multi‑kilometer esplanade will include a jogging path, bike lanes, recreational areas, and safe boundaries to prevent future encroachment along the riverbanks.

The project is fully funded by the municipality and reflects efforts to combine urban development with disaster risk reduction. Officials expect that the new public space will not only help with flood control but also promote healthier lifestyles among residents who are already using the area for walking and community activities.

Cainta Grants Land Ownership to Informal Settler Families

As part of ongoing housing reforms, the Cainta government has granted land ownership to more than 400 informal settler families, allowing them to build permanent homes and gain security of tenure. The lots are sold at acquisition cost with zero interest over an extended payment period, making it easier for low‑income families to remain in stable, legal housing.

Mayor Keith Nieto explained that this program provides long‑term independence and security for families previously living in unstable conditions along rivers and creeks. The initiative also supports flood mitigation by clearing waterways of illegal structures that had hindered drainage and increased flood risks.

Cainta Massive Community Cleanup to Address Flood Risk

In response to ongoing flooding problems, Cainta launched a massive drainage and canal cleanup program to clear obstructions and improve water flow throughout town. The initiative recruited hundreds of workers to remove debris, unclog canals, and rehabilitate drainage systems in flood‑affected subdivisions, especially along major roads like Imelda Avenue.

This cleanup drive was organized after continuous rain and severe flooding disrupted daily life, prompting greater focus on proactive flood prevention measures. Municipal officials believe that clearing clogged waterways and improving drainage will reduce the severity of future floods and protect the most vulnerable communities.
